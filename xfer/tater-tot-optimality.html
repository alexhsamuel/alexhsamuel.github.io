Tater Tot Optimality

Consider an ideal cylindrical tater tot of height `h` and radius `r`. The volume is given by,

`V = \pi r^2 h`,

while the surface area, consisting of two discs at the ends as well as a rolled up side rectangle, is given by,

`A = 2 (\pi r^2) + 2 \pi r h = 2 \pi r (r + h)`.

A tater tot is esteemed for its crispiness, which arises at the surface but not in the interior its body. We seek, therefore, to optimize the surface area for a tater tot of fixed volume `V`. In terms of this fixed parameter, we may reduce the number of free parameters to one,

`h = V / (\pi r^2)`,

in terms of which,

`A = 2 \pi r^2 + 2 \pi r V / (\pi r^2) = 2 \pi r^2 + 2 V / r`.

We now look for an extremal value by setting the derivative to zero,

`(dA)/(dr) = 4\pi r - 2 V / r^2 = 0`
`4\pi r = 2 V / r^2`
`r^3 = V / (2\pi)`
`r = (V / (2\pi))^(1/3)`.

In terms of the original parameters,

`r = ((\pi r^2 h) / (2\pi)) ^ (1/3)`
`r^3 = (r^2 h) / 2`
`r = h / 2`

In this configuration, the height of the tater tot is equal to its diameter.

To determine if this is a minimum or a maximum, we compute the second derivative at this parameter value,

`(d^2A)/(dr^2) = 4\pi + 4 V / r^3 = 4\pi + 4 V ((2\pi) / V) = 12\pi > 0`

This is therefore a relative minimum. At this point, the tatar tot of fixed volume `V` has proportions `h=2r` and achieves a pessimal surface area,

`A = 2\pi h/2 (h/2 + h) = 3/2\pi h^2 = 6\pi r^2 = root 3 (54 \pi V^2)`.

A 1 cubic cm tater tot will have a minimum surface area of approximately 5.5 square cm.

As the tater tot is either flattened out to a shorter height, or extruded to a much longer height, the area can be made arbitrary large at fixed volume. In fact,

`A = 2\pi r^2 + 2 V/r = 2 V (1/r + 1/h)`,

and since either `r` or `h` can be made arbitrary small without changing `V`, the area may be extended as much as desired.
