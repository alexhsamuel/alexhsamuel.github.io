Caves

The geological excitement continues underground. Big nearby sites include Kartchner Caverns State Park and Colossal Cave.

I love caves but haven't been to these yet.

Tombstone, AZ

"The Town Too Tough To Die"

A real Wild West mining boom town, memorialized as the site of the "Shootout at the O.K. Corral". Now exists as a Disneyfied but still fairly amusing characature of itself.

It's fun if you are open to some cheesiness.

Mining

Arizona is the Copper State and mining has been part of its entire history.

Bisbee (about 2 hours away) is a real mining town nestled among steep hills; feels a bit like a wilder mini-San Fran. Saloons, magic shows, and of course gift shops. Huge open-pit mines, some in use until the late 20th century, and mine shaft tours.

The Morenci Mine is a gigantic surface mine still in use. Have you ever seen a dump truck that is three stories tall?

Bisbee is definitely cute, like a mini San Francisco, and more real than Tombstone.

Arizona-Sonora Desert Museum

Great museum / zoo / botanical garden in the middle of the Saguaro National Forest. Cacti, of course, plus all sorts of critters: big cats, birds of prey, snakes, tarantulas, scorpions, desert tortoises, javelinas, and all sorts of others.

Fun for the whole family!

Airplane Boneyard

Pima Air & Space Museum

An airplane museum, mostly military, across from David-Monthan Air Force Base. Lots of planes, indoor and out.

I love the cheerful, compact atomic bomb right inside the entrance. And the placards all around glorify the U.S. military. Still, planes are cool.

Titan Missle Museum

A former Titan missle silo, now a museum to the Cold War.

There are also several other sealed-up Titan missle sites scattered around the area.

Scary!

Valley of the Moon

Children's fantasy park, originally privately constructed.

Never been, even though it's only a short way from home.

The clear air and isolated peaks make the Sonoran Desert great for astronomy and stargazing.

Kitt Peak National Observatory

This mountain, a couple hours outside Tucson, sprouts observatories of all shapes like mushrooms. You can visit it in the daytime, and with prior arrangement, for nighttime viewing tours.

This place is unbelievably cool and completely worth freezing your ass in the cold mountain air.

Biosphere 2

A giant greenhouse-like complex enclosing a number of biomes sealed off from the natural world (which is the putative "Biosphere 1"). Formerly used for sustainability and space survival research.

This is what it will be like to live on Mars.

Haven't been there for many, many years.

Nogales

The Mexican border is hardly more than an hour's drive south. The town of Nogales straddles the border.

The highway to Nogales, I-19, is one of the very few in the United States with signs that give distances in kilometers! Worth it for that alone.

Tucson Gem Show

Every year, end of January and beginning of February. A hobby and trade convention that leaks out to take over much of the town. Described to me by a local as "Like Burning Man with rocks."

Even outside of the Gem Show time of year, rock shops abound in Tucson.

Haven't been yet! People say that town is sort of overrun during Gem Show.

Desert

Behold the stately saguaro! They stand up to 12 meters and can live close to 200 years—they generally don't grow arms until past their 75th birthday.

  • Saguaro National Forest West lies just west of downtown, attached to Tucson Mountain Park. It's a desert gem, with hiking trails of various lengths and difficulties. Wasson Peak, a 1.5 hour, 650 meter climb, overlooks both Tucson and the park.
  • Saguaro National Forest East is disjoint but part of the same park. It lies on the far east fringes of town. Longer trails, higher peaks, and less crowd.
  • The Catalina Mountains in the Coronado National Forest form the northern border of Tucson. Many demanding trails lead up shaded canyons eventually to the forested peaks. The Sabino Canyon area is the city's most populated and best-developed hiking spot.
  • Catalina State Park on the east side of the Catalina Mountains is particularly known for wildflowers.
  • The Tortolita Mountains in Marana, a half hour drive to the north, are beautiful and lightly used.
  • Ironwood National Monument is a vast stretch of flat desert and bluffs, an hour to the northwest. It's lightly developed and great for backcountry hiking.

See AllTrails or, for a more local and back-country vibe, hikearizona.com.

The desert is bigger than it looks, and there are infinitely many places to explore, even nearby. It can also be amazingly lush, and even blooms in the spring.

Forest

The desert has trees, too! Climbing into the mountains is like driving north, except much faster. Arizona is known for sky islands, biomes isolated in high-altitude mountaintop regions. The peaks are snowcapped in the winter.

  • Mount Lemmon is Tucson's summer getaway, a beautiful drive into Catalina Mountains north of the city. Along the way, a former Japanese internment camp, now a park.
  • The Santa Rita Mountains are an hour south of Tucson. Madera Canyon and Mount Wrightson are particularly beloved, especially when Tucson is crazy hot.

Cowboys

You don't have to travel far from town to encounter ranchland. Ranchers rent grazing rights on state land, and you may encounter them or their cows munching grass among the cacti. In addition to ranchers, there are farmers, miners, drillers, geologists, hydrologists, and biologists roving the landscape.

Want to blend in? Stop by the Boot Barn and of course Arizona Hatters.

The Tucson Rodeo takes place in February.

other quirky stuff

Tucson thinks of itself as weird.

IMO, Tucson doesn't achieve a California level of weird; I'd put it somewhere around "quirky". But still fun.

Country Music

Country music and country dancing are the thing in Tucson. Locals may or may not actually be involved in agriculture, but most do drive truck-like vehicles. The dance floor is roomy and $2 drafts are still a thing. There's Two Step, there's line dancing, and there's Arizona's own special brand of Country Swing, all going down at the same time.

On special occasions, there might be a mechanical bull.

Most notably: The Maverick - King of Clubs.

Line dancing is more fun than you think it will be, especially if you deck yourself out properly; see "Cowboys".

Old Tucson

Outdoor movie studios where lots of westerns were filmed. Now a theme park of sorts. Not far from downtown, within Tucson Mountain Park.
